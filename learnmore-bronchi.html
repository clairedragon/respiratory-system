Bronchi and Bronchioles

The bronchi are two large air passages that branch off from the trachea and enter the lungs. The right bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left, which makes it more likely for inhaled objects to enter the right lung.

Inside the lungs, the bronchi further divide into smaller tubes called bronchioles. These bronchioles continue to branch into progressively smaller airways, eventually ending in clusters of tiny air sacs called alveoli, where gas exchange takes place.

The walls of the bronchi contain cartilage rings, similar to the trachea, which provide structural support and keep the airways open. However, in the bronchioles, cartilage is replaced by smooth muscle, allowing these smaller tubes to constrict or dilate to regulate airflow.

Bronchi and bronchioles are lined with ciliated epithelium and mucus-producing cells that trap and move foreign particles out of the lungs, protecting the respiratory system from infection and irritation.

These branching tubes ensure that air is efficiently distributed throughout the lungs, making them essential for effective breathing and oxygen delivery.
